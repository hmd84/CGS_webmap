Examining greenspace: the role of quality and cultural heritage

Our Work


Greenspaces are understood to be important landscape features, contributing positively to people’s mental and physical health, as well as providing space for community connections to be created and grow. We have created a web map in which you can explore the cultural heritage and quality of selected greenspaces across Edinburgh. We also invite you to provide feedback on the benefits you experience whilst visiting greenspaces, to improve understanding of the diverse ways in which greenspaces positively contribute to society.


Cultural Heritage Assessment


Cultural heritage is often neglected in greenspace studies; however, it is one of the services that ecosystems (including greenspace) can provide to humans (collectively termed ‘Cultural Ecosystem Services’).

Cultural heritage results from human interactions with and the shaping of the natural environment (Millennium Ecosystem Assessment, 2005). It is important to understand existing cultural heritage features, as they can provide people with a connection to the past, as well as provide a sense of community (Dallimer et al. 2015).

Greenspace Quality Assessment


Public access to greenspace is an important driver for its usage, however it is also crucial to evaluate the quality of these spaces as poor-quality greenspace can discourage use (Hoffimann et al, 2017).

Through exploring the presence and quality of particular greenspace features typically found within Scotland, we can begin to understand the quality of Edinburgh’s greenspaces.


Jewel Park

Map


Our map displays the data collected during our investigation that was used for input into an Environmental Deprivation Model.

Greenspace quality was assessed at 8 parks in Edinburgh, generating a 'POST' ranking for each park that could be compared to other greenspace evaluations with a broader scope.

The map also displays the individual ratings calculated for each Edinburgh Datazone (based on the SIMD datazone boundaries) for each of the Environmental Deprivation Model (EDM) inputs.

The decision process behind which zone characteristics were included is detailed further in the Work
section of the site.
