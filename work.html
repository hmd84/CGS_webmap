Our Work

We have quantitatively assessed both the cultural heritage value and quality of selected greenspaces across Edinburgh.

Our team have collated numerous datasets, performed spatial analysis and ground-truthed greenspace quality to bring you the data displayed on our web map. Whilst the focus for our investigation was the cultural and historical values of Edinburgh's greenspaces our report focused on the creation of an "Environmental Deprivation Model" (EDM) for Edinburgh. Such models are valuable for assessing how the quality and provision of services has an impact on the wellbeing of a population. However, during the course of our investigation it became clear to us that to create an EDM for Edinburgh we would require a much more comprehensive and thorough investigation. For this reason, the data we present to you now does not include our overall EDM calculations as we felt these were not truly representative of the city.

Please scroll down this page for further information for the adopted methods, results and conclusions of this study.

Cultural Heritage Assessment

Database


We collected seven datasets from Historic Environment Scotland, consisting of the following historic and cultural features:

  • Listed Buildings,


  • Scheduled Monuments,


  • Gardens and Designed Landscapes,


  • Battlefields,


  • World Heritage Sites,


  • Properties in Care,


  • National Record of the Historic Environment (Canmore Points including Maritime).


    • Method


    Cultural Heritage Index (CHI)

    In our study, cultural heritage has been assessed by the spatial distribution of specific elements in the physical landscape that have historical and cultural association, listed above.


    A cultural heritage index (CHI) was calculated by multiplying the density and diversity of these elements across greenspaces in Edinburgh, adapting the method used by Stanik et al (2018).

    Results


    The spread of cultural heritage index values shows that greenspaces to the south-east and north-west of the city centre have the highest index values, whereas greenspaces in the south-west of the city have almost no cultural heritage features present. Further away from the city centre, greenspaces outside of the main urban area generally have a low index score.

    Greenspace Quality Assessment

    Method


    Assessment of Public Open Spaces (POS), and by extension greenspaces, is a growing area of research due to increasing recognition of the value quality open spaces provides to the public.


    The Public Open Spaces Toolkit (POST) was used to assess quality of selected greenspaces within Edinburgh. This toolkit was developed by the University of Western Australia and adapted by us for use within a Scottish context. POST assesses quality across four themes: Activity provision, Environmental Quality, Amenities, and Safety.

    The assessments were carried out on site across eight different greenspaces within Edinburgh. These greenspaces were selected for their diversity in cultural heritage values, and range in socioeconomic deprivation rankings of surrounding neighbourhoods.

    Database


    The results from the POST quality assessment for the eight parks were compared against the following datasets:

  • Green Flag Quality Assessment (2019),


  • Open Space Audit (2016).


    • This comparison enabled us to ground-truth the results from the above datasets.

    Results


    The POST assessment showed greater agreement with the Green Flag Quality Assessment (2019) therefore the Green Flag quality ratings for selected greenspaces have been included in our web map. The Green Flag results show that most neighbourhoods (defined by Scottish Index of Multiple Deprivation (SIMD) boundaries) areas have access to greenspaces scoring above the minimum quality standard of 60%, a target set by Edinburgh Council (2019).

    Conclusion and Discussion

    Our findings show that whilst most neighbourhoods have access to greenspaces achieving the minimum quality standard, greenspace cultural heritage scores vary spatially across the City of Edinburgh.


    However, cultural heritage is only one type of benefit that greenspaces have to offer. There is a whole range of cultural ecosystem services, and the diversity and richness of these benefits, and their spatial variation across Edinburgh’s greenspaces, should also be explored.


    This will help deepen understanding of the role and importance of greenspaces to Edinburgh’s population.
